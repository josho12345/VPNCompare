Does My Phone Support eSIM? The 2-Minute Check

Published 19 July 2026 · Facts verified at publication · 4 min read

Before you buy any travel eSIM, one question matters more than price, data, or coverage: can your phone actually use it? Plenty of travellers buy a plan at the airport gate, scan the QR code, and discover their phone has no idea what an eSIM is. Here are the checks that settle it in under two minutes — including the two traps that catch people with "compatible" phones.

The universal check: dial a code

Works on almost every phone

Open your phone dialler and type: *#06#

No call needed — the screen responds instantly. If the list that appears includes an EID (a 32-digit number), your phone has eSIM hardware. No EID listed? No eSIM support, full stop.

The menu check — iPhone

  1. Open Settings › Mobile Data (or Cellular in some regions).
  2. Look for Add eSIM or Add Data Plan.
  3. If the option is there, you're eSIM-ready.

As a rule of thumb, every iPhone from the XS, XR (2018) onward supports eSIM — but read the regional trap below before you relax.

The menu check — Android

  1. Open Settings › Connections (Samsung) or Network & Internet (Pixel and most others).
  2. Tap SIM Manager / SIMs.
  3. Look for Add eSIM or Download a SIM instead?

Broadly: Pixel 3 and newer, Samsung Galaxy S20 and newer, and most flagships from 2020 on support eSIM. Budget and mid-range Androids are a coin flip even today — which is exactly why the dial-code check exists.

Trap one: carrier lock

Compatible hardware, blocked anyway

A phone bought on contract can be carrier-locked — the hardware supports eSIM, but only your carrier's. iPhone: Settings › General › About › look for "No SIM restrictions". Locked phone? Ask your carrier to unlock it before your trip — most must do it free once the handset is paid off, but it can take days, so don't leave it for the departure lounge.

Trap two: the regional model catch

The one nobody mentions: the same phone model can ship with different SIM hardware in different countries. Best-known example — iPhones sold in mainland China have two physical SIM slots and no eSIM at all, and some US models are the opposite: eSIM-only with no physical slot. Samsung has regional variants too. This is why "iPhone 14 supports eSIM" isn't a guarantee — your iPhone 14 is what the dial code checks. Trust the EID, not the spec sheet.

Phone passed? Here's what that unlocks

An eSIM-ready phone means you can land with data already working: plan bought at home, QR scanned on your sofa, activated when the wheels touch down. No airport kiosk, no tiny plastic tray, and your home SIM stays in for calls and banking texts.
