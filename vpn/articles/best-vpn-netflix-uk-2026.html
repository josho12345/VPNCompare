Why Netflix Blocks VPNs in the First Place

Netflix is contractually obligated by content licensors to enforce regional restrictions on its catalogue. When Netflix detects an IP address it recognises as belonging to a VPN provider, it blocks the connection or shows a proxy error rather than serving any content. This is not a technical limitation on Netflix's part — it is a deliberate enforcement mechanism written into their licensing agreements with studios and broadcasters.

The consequence for VPN users is straightforward: most VPNs simply do not work reliably with Netflix any more. Providers that do work tend to actively maintain and rotate dedicated streaming server IP pools specifically to stay ahead of Netflix's detection systems. This is why our testing focused only on VPNs that maintain this kind of active streaming infrastructure rather than every VPN on the market.

How We Tested

We connected to UK servers from each VPN and attempted to load Netflix UK content directly, checking for proxy errors, buffering, and successful playback in HD. Netflix typically requires around 15 Mbps for Ultra HD playback — any VPN that drops below this threshold on a UK server is unsuitable for serious streaming use regardless of whether it technically unblocks the library.

What to Look For in a Netflix VPN

Beyond whether a VPN simply unblocks Netflix at the moment of testing, a few factors determine whether it remains reliable over time. Server network size and freshness matter, since Netflix actively blocks known VPN IP addresses and providers need to rotate or add new ones to stay ahead. Speed retention on the UK server specifically matters more than overall global speed, since that is the server you will actually be connecting to. Device allowance matters if more than one person in your household streams simultaneously. And a genuine money-back guarantee matters because Netflix's blocking behaviour can change at any time without warning.
